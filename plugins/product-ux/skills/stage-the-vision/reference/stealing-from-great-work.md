# Stealing from great work

The honest lever. A model is **not a native visual artist** — asked to originate art from its own head, it produces the statistical average, which is the generic and the trendy. The reliable path to art is not "be more creative." It is to **study specific great work and adapt its moves** — the way every real artist learns. Originate less; adapt excellence more.

## Why adaptation beats origination here

Grounding leans on external *reality* so the look isn't generic. This is the same instinct aimed at *excellence*: lean on external *great work* so the art is actually good. The model can't invent at the level of a master designer, but it *can* recognize, decompose, and adapt what masters did. Standing on real shoulders is not a shortcut around art — it is how art is made.

## What to study

- **Real, exemplary, *specific* work** — award-winning sites (the kind that win Awwwards/FWA, studio work like the recognized interactive houses), real graphic design, real art and photography, film and title sequences, editorial layout. Named artifacts, not "minimalist vibe."
- **Work adjacent to this feeling**, not this category — for a feeling of quiet intimacy, study whoever does quiet intimacy *brilliantly* in any medium, not "other coaching sites."
- **The subject's own material when it exists** (conditional, not required) — and the real references gathered for grounding (`grounding-restraint-and-the-taste-gate.md`).

## How to steal well (reverse-engineer the moves)

Don't copy the surface — extract the *move*:

- **What makes it stop you?** Name the specific device: the scale of the type, the single dramatic image, the restraint of the palette, the one motion, the use of space, the unexpected crop.
- **Why does it work?** The principle under the device (focal dominance, contrast, negative space, a bold concept held with restraint).
- **Transpose, don't trace.** Apply the *move* to *this* feeling and content — never lift the artifact. "They let one huge image carry the whole hero with almost no text" → adapt the *strategy* (one dominant image, radical text restraint) to your concept, in your world. Copying the artifact is theft; adapting the move is craft (and avoids both genericness and plagiarism).
- **Combine sources.** A composition move from one, a motion idea from another, a colour discipline from a third — synthesized into something that is *this* project's, not any one source's.

## Grounding is a *process*: retrieve → decompose to structure → critique against

"Steal from great work" is not a vibe — it is a concrete method with three steps, and skipping any one collapses it back into priors:

1. **Retrieve a specific exemplar.** Name *1–3 real pieces that nailed THIS kind of moment* — this hero, this transition, this kind of immersive scene — not the category, not a style label. A named artifact you can point at, or you have nothing to adapt.
2. **Decompose to STRUCTURE, not surface.** Pull out each exemplar's *structural and conceptual* moves: the organizing idea, the hierarchy (what leads, what recedes), the use of space and type, the one move that *breaks the template*. **Mark its surface treatment as do-NOT-copy** — its palette, its mascot/character, its typeface, its specific effects are the *noise*; lift none of them. Structure adapted becomes taste; surface copied becomes a knock-off. Decompose the skeleton; discard the skin.
3. **Critique the output AGAINST the exemplar.** When the moment is staged, hold it up beside the pieces you retrieved: does it have the *structural* quality that made them stop you, or only borrowed paint? The exemplar is the bar the result is judged against, not just the seed it grew from.

**Keep a curated library of decomposed exemplars.** The durable form of this skill is a growing set of *real pieces already decomposed to their structural moves* (idea, hierarchy, space, type, the template-break — surface explicitly marked off). Retrieving from a maintained library beats re-finding references cold every time, and keeps grounding standing on the same shoulders across a whole body of work.

## Keep it honest

- **Adapt moves, not assets.** Don't reproduce someone's actual imagery, illustration, or copyrighted work; learn the technique and make your own (or source/commission real assets — `realize`'s build-vs-source).
- **Transpose to the feeling.** A move stolen and dropped in unchanged is just a different trend. It must serve *this* concept or it doesn't earn its place.
- **Name your references.** Record what you studied and what you took, so the Staging's choices are explicable and reviewable (`architect-mentality` — preserve understanding).

## Pitfalls

- **Inventing from priors** — skipping this step and "being creative," i.e. defaulting to the average. The original failure.
- **Category-studying** — looking at "other sites like this" (which are themselves generic) instead of genuinely great work in the relevant *feeling*.
- **Tracing the surface** — copying an artifact's look instead of extracting and transposing its move; produces a knock-off, not art.
- **A single source** — leaning on one reference so hard the result is derivative; synthesize several.
- **Style-label laundering** — "make it editorial/cinematic" instead of *this specific piece* did *this specific thing* I'm adapting.
- **Copying surface instead of structure** — lifting an exemplar's palette/character/typeface/effects (its *surface*) rather than its organizing idea, hierarchy, and template-break (its *structure*); the surface is the noise, the structure is the taste.
- **Grounding as a vibe, not a process** — invoking "inspired by great work" without the three steps (retrieve a named piece → decompose to structure → critique the output against it); a feeling of grounding with none of the method.
