# Inventing the concept

The hardest, most valuable, least-specified move: turning a *feeling* into a single **bold visual idea** — the image you'd screenshot, the thing that makes someone stop. Everything else in the Staging serves the concept. This is the generative act grounding can't supply and the safe-default reflex destroys.

## The concept is one striking image, not a layout

A concept is not "a clean hero with a headline and two buttons." It is the **organizing visual idea** — the feeling made into a single image or moment:

- feeling "you can finally exhale" → *a vast, near-empty field of quiet space with one luminous element that breathes; the whole screen is the held breath, slowly releasing.*
- feeling "from survival's dark toward thriving's light" → *you descend through a room where the light turns from dusk to dawn as you scroll, a figure resolving from silhouette to clarity.*
- feeling "raw, untamed power" → *a lion's face fills the hero; as you scroll it turns to meet your eyes.*

Each is *one idea* the whole screen obeys. Name yours in a sentence. If you can't, you have a layout, not a concept.

## Generate bold, then choose true

A model's first idea is its most average — the statistical safe centre. So don't take it. The method:

1. **Generate several *daring* options** — deliberately reach past the safe one. Push each toward "too much," toward a real image, a real metaphor, a real *moment*. Three bold concepts beat one competent one because they give you something to choose between and graft from.
2. **Choose the most striking that is *still true*** — true to the feeling and to the human in the moment (a bold idea that betrays a grief app's tenderness is wrong, however striking). Striking *and* true is the bar; either alone fails.
3. **Then restrain the rendering** — the bold *idea*, rendered with discipline (`grounding-restraint-and-the-taste-gate.md`). Bold ≠ loud: the concept is daring; its execution is controlled.

## Metaphor made visual

The Vision usually hands you a metaphor in words ("a home," "the turning light," "a path through a garden"). Your job is to make it a concrete *visual*, not leave it a mood:

- Take the metaphor literally as a *scene*: if it's "a home," what is the room, the light, the threshold, the thing on the mantel?
- Find the *one frame* that captures it — the establishing shot of this world.
- Then find the *moment* — the thing that happens (the light turns, the figure looks up, the door opens) that makes it move and live.

## From concept to moments

The concept is the whole; the **moments** are how it plays across the screen and the scroll — the hero, the reveal, the transitions, the living details (`composition-and-cinematic-staging.md`). Invent the concept first; the moments are its scenes. A screen with no concept is a pile of moments that don't add up; a concept with no moments is a poster that never moves.

## Inventing isn't only visual — invent the interactions too

The same genius that invents a striking *image* invents striking *interactions and controls* — how the user acts, not only what they see. When a data type or an action is painful through standard controls, inventing a novel control (a pull-to-adjust handle, a control that springs back to centre) is as much *the art* as any hero shot. This is the ping-pong with use: `astonishing-to-use`'s **facilitating-input** cluster identifies the input friction and demands a better control; you invent its artful, physical *form*; the use is then validated. Bold inventions of interaction are part of the genius — make them, then make them genuinely usable.

## Pitfalls

- **Settling for the first, safe idea** — the model's average; competent, forgettable.
- **A "concept" that's really a layout** — hero + cards described in nicer words; no organizing image.
- **Striking but untrue** — spectacle that betrays the feeling or the human (drama on a page that needed tenderness).
- **Metaphor left as mood** — "a warm, homey feel" instead of *this* room, *this* light, *this* moment.
- **No single owner idea** — five cool moments, no concept tying them into one image. The screen feels busy and generic at once.
