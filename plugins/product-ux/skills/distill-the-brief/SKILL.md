# Distill the Brief

Before a great architect draws a single line, they sit with the client and listen to a mess: a pile of wants, magazine clippings, a few half-sketched solutions, a contradiction or two, a budget, and a feeling the client can't quite name. The architect's first work is not to draw. It is to find the **program** — what this building must *do*, for whom, what it must *feel* like, what is genuinely required and what was merely one person's idea of *how* — and to decide what is *one building* and what is *a campus*. Only when the problem is clear, true, and rightly sized does design begin.

**This skill is that first work — for interfaces.** You are handed reality as it actually arrives: a request, a dossier, research, a product concept, a transcript, a vague line. None of it is yet a design problem `envision-the-experience` can take. Your job is to **distil** it into one — or into a small, coherent *tree* of them — at the right altitude, with the solution stripped out, correctly sized, so the visioning that follows imagines a world to solve the *real* problem rather than transcribing someone's half-formed answer.

You are not designing anything here. You are deciding **what is to be designed, and what the real problem is.** Get that wrong and everything downstream is wasted craft on the wrong question.

---

## The one move: distil the problem, never the solution

Everything here reduces to a single discipline. **Hand `envision` the problem. Never the solution.**

A dossier always arrives carrying its own answers. A product concept says "hold a soft door open for one breath." A stakeholder says "we need a dashboard." A spec says "a three-step wizard." These are *solutions* — someone already leapt from the need to a form. They feel like requirements because they're written down, but they are guesses about *how*, smuggled in next to the *what*.

If you pass those guesses through as the brief, you have quietly answered the design question before the designer ever sees it. `envision`'s whole power is to ask "what is this element, truly, in *this* world?" — and it cannot ask that of a brief that has already decreed the element. You will have turned **design into transcription**, and the result will be exactly the generic, off-the-feeling output that made someone reach for these skills in the first place.

So you run it the other way:

- The concept says **"a soft door held for one breath."** Under it: *the person is about to ask for something and it feels like weakness; the design must make naming a need feel safe and small, not like a form or a confession.* That — the ache and what the moment must do — is the brief. Whether it becomes a held door, a whispered field, a single breath of space, or something no one has built is **`envision`'s to discover**.
- The stakeholder says **"a dashboard."** Under it: *who needs to know what, in what moment, to decide or act — and what is the one thing they actually came for.* That is the brief. Maybe it's a dashboard. Maybe it's a single sentence and one number. The word "dashboard" was a pointer to a need, not an instruction.

**Distilling is subtraction of the answer and recovery of the question.** You keep the ache, the intent, the feeling, the genuine functional requirements, and the load-bearing constraints. You drop the form. (`architect-mentality`: *build for the goal, not the brief* — the literal words are a pointer to an outcome.)

---

## Find the altitude

A brief is at the right altitude when it is a **design problem**: enough to constrain the design honestly, not so much that it dictates the design, not so little that it's an untethered vibe. Intake almost never arrives there. It arrives too low or too high, and your job is to bring it to the line.

- **Too low — a solution in disguise.** Whole layouts, named components, prescribed flows, a wall of micro-decisions. Lift it: for each prescribed *how*, ask *what need was that serving?* and keep the need. (The 38-page concept, the Figma-in-words, the "just build what we have but better.")
- **Too high — a vibe with no problem.** "Make it delightful." "An app for couples." "Something modern." There's a feeling but no graspable problem, no user-in-a-moment, no requirements. Lower it: find the specific human, the specific ache, the specific things they must be able to *do*, the concrete context. Ground the vibe in a real situation until it has edges.

The test of altitude: **could `envision` imagine more than one genuinely different world from this brief, and could each be judged true or false against it?** If only one design satisfies the brief, you're too low (you wrote the answer). If *anything* satisfies it, you're too high (you wrote a mood). The right brief is a problem with room — and a backbone.

---

## Decide the grain — and model the englobing context

A second decision sits beside altitude: **how big is one brief?**

One brief is **one design problem** — one experience `envision` can hold whole in a single mind and produce one coherent Vision for; in app terms, roughly one surface or one charged moment you could screenshot and judge. An intake may be exactly one such problem (a single screen, a lone flow) — then distilling is light: strip, ground, hand it on. But a large intake — a whole app, a product concept, a multi-feature module — is **many** design problems wearing one cover, and the central act of this skill is to cut it at the joints.

Cutting at the joints is not slicing into arbitrary pieces. It is two moves held together:

**1. Split into surfaces along real seams.** Find where the experience genuinely changes character — by practice, by moment, by mission (a playful corner, a tender one, a transactional one, a crisis one). Each seam yields a **surface brief**: a self-contained design problem `envision` can vision on its own. Name what is one and what is many, and *why* the cut falls there.

**2. Model the englobing context — and design it *for* its children.** Surfaces are never a flat list of unrelated screens. There is an **app / world / module that contains them**, and that container is *itself a design problem* — often the most important one. Its brief is not "a nav bar"; it is the **spine and the world the surfaces inhabit**: the through-line feeling that makes them one product, how a person moves between them, what is shared across them (an identity, a timeline, a state), how the whole *composes* — and how it behaves as one thing (when it recedes, when it bridges, when it foregrounds a surface). Crucially, the context brief is framed **with its children in mind**: it exists to make them cohere, so you write it knowing what must live inside it, never bottom-up from one room. (`architect-mentality`: *model the missing concept* — the container is a real concept with its own contract, not the gap between the screens; *concentrate complexity so the edges stay simple* — the context holds what's shared so each surface stays clean.)

This is the move a flat decomposition misses and the reason splitting doesn't fragment: **you emit a tree, not a list.** A context at the root, the surfaces native to it as children, each child knowing the world it belongs to. A surface designed in ignorance of its context becomes a screen from a different app; a context designed in ignorance of its surfaces becomes an empty shell. The tree keeps them coherent — one world, many native inhabitants.

(How deep the tree goes is itself a grain decision: a small app may be one context and a handful of surfaces; a large one may have sub-contexts — a module that is a world within the world. Cut only as fine as the design problems genuinely differ. Over-splitting manufactures coordination cost; under-splitting hands `envision` more than one mind can hold.)

## Keep only the load-bearing constraints

A dossier carries constraints the way a beach carries sand: a list of commandments, principles, must-nots, brand rules, research findings, edge cases. Pour all of it into a brief and you bury the design problem — `envision` cannot imagine freely inside a cage of forty rules, and most of them don't even bear on *this* surface.

For each brief, carry only the constraints that are **load-bearing for this design problem**: the few that would make a design *wrong* if violated — the ones that shape the form, protect the user, or are the non-negotiable truth of the product. A constraint that is really a *solution* ("use a bottom sheet") gets stripped like any other solution. A constraint that belongs to a different surface goes to that surface. A constraint that's a nice-to-have is dropped or noted as soft.

The discipline: **a brief should feel like the fewest true things that fully frame the problem.** If you can remove a line and no design becomes wrong, remove it. (`architect-mentality`: *refuse false tradeoffs* — a brief both unconstrained enough to imagine in and constrained enough to be honest.)

## The Brief Tree — what this skill produces

The deliverable is a **Brief Tree**: an englobing **context** brief at the root and the **surface** briefs native to it as children (going deeper only where the problems genuinely differ). Each node is a clean **envision-input** — a design problem at the right altitude, solution-free.

A single **brief** (context or surface) names, in whatever prose conveys it best — *not* a rigid form:

- **The design problem.** In a line or two: what this surface or context is *for*, in this product and this moment. The question, not the answer.
- **Who it's for, and the ache.** The specific human in the specific moment, and the real pain or need under it — and the passion beneath the pain (what they actually want to be true).
- **The feeling.** The one emotional truth this place should carry. (This is the seed `envision` grows into a world — give it honestly, but do *not* design the world.)
- **The Lead Force.** Which of the two co-equal forces leads *this* node — the **use** (app-y: UX-led, restraint, effortless task-flow) or the **art** (dreamy: concept-led, immersion, a striking world) — and how far. Decide it from the real drivers, *never* from the kind of app: the **job in the moment** (accomplish a task under load vs. be moved/persuaded/immersed), the **stakes** (money, safety, grief, conflict pull toward clarity; brand, discovery, celebration allow immersion), the **frequency** (a daily tool earns restraint; a once-seen page can spend spectacle), and the **device & ergonomics** (thumb-bound, hurried, in glare). State it in a line with its *why* — e.g. *"use-led (app-y) — daily, thumb-bound, can arrive mid-conflict; the art elevates but never taxes the use."* This records `astonishing-to-use`'s "the mission sets who leads" at the right altitude, so the dream↔use balance is set once here instead of re-litigated every version downstream. (See the caution below: app *type* is a prior, never a lookup; and the lead may differ *within* a node.)
- **The functional requirements.** What a person must be able to *do* here, and the data and states involved — as needs, never as widgets ("name a need and send it", not "a text field and a button").
- **The load-bearing constraints.** Only the few that would make a design wrong — the form-shaping, user-protecting, product-defining truths. No more.
- **Explicitly out of scope — including the form.** What this brief does *not* cover (it belongs to a sibling, or to the context), and the standing exclusion that no solution is prescribed: **the form is `envision`'s to invent.**

For a **context** brief, "the functional requirements" is the **spine and the world its children inhabit**: the through-line feeling, how surfaces are reached and moved between, what is shared across them, how the whole composes and behaves as one — authored *with the children in view*, and naming the children it contains. It describes the *world*, never the children's internals (those are their own briefs).

**On the Lead Force — app type is a prior, never a rule.** "Functional/mobile-first → app-y, landing page/portfolio → dreamy" is a useful *gut prior*, but the kind of app does **not** determine the lead and must never be used as a lookup: the same type spans the whole axis (Amazon vs. a perfume house are both "shopping"; Wordle vs. *Journey* are both "games"; a dev's text blog vs. an NYT photo-essay are both "blogs"). What actually decides it is the job, the stakes, the frequency, and the device — which cut *across* types. And the lead is **not one coarse dial**: it can differ surface to surface across the tree (a couples app's *home* may be art-led while its *conflict-repair* surface is use-led), and even *within* one surface (a **dreamy heart can wear an app-y shell** — an immersive world whose *wayfinding* is a calm, findable app shell). Set it per node, justified, and let it compose.

A Brief Tree names **no implementation and no form** — same discipline `envision` keeps for the Vision, one stage earlier. You hand each brief to **`envision-the-experience`**: the context brief becomes the world the surfaces are envisioned inside; each surface brief becomes its own Vision, native to that world. **Problem → feeling → art → build.**

## How to distil an intake

1. **Read for the real problem, not the proposed answer.** Go through the whole intake once asking, of everything written down, *is this a need or a guess at how to meet it?* Mark the solutions for stripping. Mark the genuine needs, aches, feelings, requirements, and hard constraints for keeping.

2. **Find the user(s) and the ache(s).** Behind a pile of features is a human in a moment with a pain. Name them specifically. If the intake is all features and no human, that's the first thing to recover — a brief with no user-in-a-moment is too high.

3. **Decide the grain — list the surfaces and find their seams.** Where does the experience genuinely change character? Cut there. Name each surface as a design problem. Note what you deliberately keep as one, and why.

4. **Model the englobing context.** Name the world/app/module that holds the surfaces — its through-line feeling, its spine (what's shared, how you move, how it composes and behaves as one) — framed *for* the children you just found. This is a brief in its own right, usually the most important. If there's only one surface, the context may be trivial or absent; say so.

5. **Distil each node to its brief.** For the context and each surface: state the design problem, the user and ache, the one feeling, the **Lead Force** (use-led / art-led / balanced, with its *why* from the job/stakes/frequency/device), the functional requirements (as needs), and *only* the load-bearing constraints. Strip every solution and every form. State what's out.

6. **Check altitude and coherence on every node.** Could `envision` imagine several true-yet-different worlds from this brief (not too low), and would some designs be plainly wrong against it (not too high)? Does each surface know its context, and does the context account for each surface? Does the tree, read top to bottom, describe *one product* — or did a surface wander into a different app?

7. **Hand the tree to `envision`.** Context first (the world), then each surface as an inhabitant of it.

When in doubt about a cut, an altitude, or whether something is a need or a solution, **surface the ambiguity to the user rather than guessing** — distilling wrong is expensive, because everything downstream trusts the brief. (`architect-mentality`: *design for the consumer*; resolve ambiguity by reasoning over the real context, and ask when the context can't settle it.)

## Ask yourself

- Did I hand `envision` the **problem**, or did I smuggle in a **solution** the dossier had already guessed? For every "how" in the intake, did I recover the "what" under it and drop the "how"?
- Is each brief at the right **altitude** — could `envision` imagine more than one genuinely different world from it (not too low), and could a design be judged *wrong* against it (not too high)?
- Did I decide the **grain** on purpose — naming what is one design problem and what is many, and cutting the surfaces at real seams (where the experience changes character), not arbitrary slices?
- Did I **model the englobing context as its own design problem**, framed *for* its children — its feeling, spine, and composition — rather than leaving a flat list of unrelated screens?
- Does every surface **know its context**, and does the context **account for every surface**? Read top to bottom, does the tree describe **one product**?
- Did I set each node's **Lead Force** (use-led / art-led / balanced) from the real drivers — the *job, stakes, frequency, device* — and *not* from the kind of app, letting it differ across (and within) surfaces rather than applying one flat dial?
- Did I carry **only the load-bearing constraints** — the few that would make a design wrong — and drop the rest, so `envision` can imagine freely?
- Is every brief **free of form and implementation** — does it leave the design genuinely open, the way a Vision leaves the build open?
- Did I **ground** a too-high vibe in a real human, ache, and context, and **lift** a too-low solution back to the need it served?
- For anything I was unsure of — a cut, an altitude call, a need-vs-solution judgment — did I **surface it** instead of quietly deciding it for everyone downstream?

## Red flags

- A brief that **names the form** — components, layouts, flows, a "wizard", a "dashboard", "a soft door" — handed to `envision` as if it were the requirement. Design has been done before the designer arrived.
- The whole dossier **passed through** as the brief — its solutions, its forty constraints, its edge cases — burying the design problem so deep `envision` can only transcribe it.
- A brief that's a **vibe** ("make it delightful", "an app for couples") with no user-in-a-moment, no ache, no requirements — too high to design against.
- A brief that's a **spec dump** — every micro-decision pre-made — too low, with room for exactly one design (the one already written).
- A big intake **flattened into one giant brief**, or sliced into **arbitrary pieces** that don't fall on real seams.
- Surfaces split out as a **flat list with no englobing context** — no world holding them, so each gets designed as if from a different app.
- A **context brief that's really a nav bar** — "a tab bar and a header" — instead of the spine and world its children inhabit, framed for them.
- A **Lead Force read off the app *type*** ("it's a shop, so app-y") instead of the job/stakes/frequency/device — or one balance applied flatly across a whole tree whose surfaces genuinely differ, or treated as a single overall dial when a dreamy heart could wear an app-y shell.
- A surface brief written in **ignorance of its context** (a screen from another product), or a context written in **ignorance of its surfaces** (an empty shell).
- **Constraints that are solutions** ("use a bottom sheet") kept as if they were requirements.
- Quietly **resolving a real ambiguity** (a cut, an altitude, a need-vs-solution call) by guessing, when the right move was to ask — and everything downstream inherited the guess.

> **Origin.** This skill is the **upstream, framing** member of the experience-design **quartet**: it decides **what is to be designed and what the real problem is** before anything imagines how it should feel, look, or be built. It turns messy intake into a **Brief Tree** — an englobing context and the surfaces native to it — and hands each node to `product-ux:envision-the-experience` (which imagines the world/feeling for one problem → the Vision), then `product-ux:stage-the-vision` (the bold web-native art → the Staging), then `product-ux:realize-the-vision` (the build). **Problem → feeling → art → build**, on one continuous ladder: distil speaks *design problems* (no form), `envision` speaks *sensory feeling* (no implementation), `stage` speaks *web-native art*, `realize` speaks *engineering* — each stage cleanly separated from the next.

> **Related.** It is the anti-anchoring sibling of `product-ux:redesign-means-rethink`: that skill refuses to reverse-engineer the new design from *old code*; this one refuses to carry the solution in from the *incoming dossier* — both insist the *form* has no authority, only the *intent and requirements* do. Where `envision` reads "*build for the goal, not the brief*" at the level of a single element (the spec's "menu" is a pointer to a need), this skill reads it at the level of the **whole intake** (the dossier's answers are pointers to problems). And it is a direct expression of `engineering:architect-mentality`: *build for the goal not the brief* (recover intent from literal words), *model the missing concept* (the englobing context as a designed thing with its own contract), *concentrate complexity so the edges stay simple* (the context holds what's shared; each surface stays a clean problem), *refuse false tradeoffs* (a brief both open and honest), and *design for the consumer* (`envision` is the consumer of your brief — hand it a problem it can imagine freely inside).
