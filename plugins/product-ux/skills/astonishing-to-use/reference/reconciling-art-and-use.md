# Reconciling art & use

The crux: where the **artful choice** and the **easy choice** pull apart — and how to make them serve each other instead of fight. This is the practical machinery of the ping-pong. The art must *elevate* the use, never *tax* it; when they conflict, you don't sacrifice the use — you **invent** the design that gives both.

## The art-vs-use conflicts (spot them)

These are the recurring ways a beautiful concept hurts the use. Each is a place the ping-pong must fire:

- **Scroll-jacking / hijacked scroll** — a cinematic scroll that fights the user's natural scrolling, traps them, or makes the page feel broken. The art took control of the user's most basic gesture.
- **Transitions that delay every action** — a gorgeous 800ms animation between the user and *every* result. Stunning once, infuriating by the tenth tap.
- **Motion between the user and their goal** — anything they must *wait through* to act. Animation should cover real work or guide attention, never gate the task.
- **Hidden / artful navigation** — a nav so creatively reimagined that people can't find it or don't recognize it as nav (the "sunflower menu" that's beautiful and unusable).
- **Buried primary action** — the hero, the spectacle, the full-screen art pushes the actual thing the user came to do below the fold or out of sight.
- **Form over signifier** — art flattens everything into beautiful sameness, so nothing reads as tappable (mystery-meat; see `clarity-and-cognitive-load.md`).
- **Spectacle tax** — heavy effects that drain battery, jank on real phones, or make the page slow to load (use cost disguised as beauty).
- **Impress-once, annoy-forever** — the test for any flourish: delightful the first time, obstructive every time after. If so, it's a gimmick, not craft.

## How to dissolve them (refuse the tradeoff)

The conflict is almost never fundamental — it's a *framing*. The move is to find the design where the art *is* the easy path:

- **Make the art carry the function.** The best resolutions are where the striking moment *also* does a job — the cinematic reveal *is* the navigation; the beautiful transition *covers* a real load; the bold hero *contains* the primary action rather than burying it. Art that serves the task earns its place; art that obstructs it doesn't.
- **Demote, don't delete (usually).** Often the fix is keeping the artful idea but making it optional, faster, skippable, or secondary to the task path — the spectacle on the *way* to the goal, not *across* it.
- **Re-conceive at the concept level.** When a moment genuinely can't be made usable, the fix belongs back in `stage-the-vision` / `envision-the-experience`: a *different* bold idea that's striking *and* effortless. That is the ping-pong's return volley — not a quiet UX compromise on the existing idea.
- **If it truly can't be both, the use wins** — unless the mission is declared art-over-use (a pure art piece). State that exception explicitly; never let it be the silent default.

## Running the ping-pong (concretely)

1. Take the concept (a staged moment, a flow).
2. Run the **four questions** and scan the conflict list above — mark every place the art makes the user work, wait, guess, hunt, or fight.
3. For each, try to dissolve it by making the art *carry* the function; if you can't, send it back to be **re-conceived**, not quietly nerfed.
4. Re-pressure the new version. Repeat until both forces are great and neither improves without harming the other.
5. The mission sets who leads each round (see the SKILL); the loop itself is non-negotiable.

## Pitfalls

- **Treating beauty-vs-ease as a real tradeoff** — accepting the first compromise instead of inventing the both-design.
- **Silent UX sacrifice** — protecting the art by quietly making the use worse, with no one deciding that on purpose.
- **One-and-done** — pressuring the concept once instead of volleying until both are great.
- **Gimmick blindness** — keeping a flourish that fails the impress-once/annoy-forever test because it demos well.
- **Forgetting the partner failure** — over-correcting into safe, generic, easy-but-forgettable; the answer is *both* great, never trading one for the other.
