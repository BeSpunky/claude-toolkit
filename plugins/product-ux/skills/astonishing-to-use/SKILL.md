# Astonishing to Use

A design can be breathtaking in a screenshot and miserable in the hand. It can win a beauty contest and lose the user — too many taps to the thing they came for, a nav they can't find, a scroll that fights them, a transition that delays every action, a form that bores them into leaving. **Looking astonishing and being astonishing to use are different achievements, and great design needs both.**

The experience-design trio is built to make things astonishing to *look at and feel* — the feeling, the bold art, the staged moments. **This skill is the co-equal force it was missing: making things astonishing to *use*.** Not "usable enough." Astonishing — the kind of use that feels effortless, obvious, fast, and quietly delightful, the kind people don't notice because nothing ever got in their way.

The test is four plain questions, asked of the real person on their real device:

1. **Does it make life easy?** — fewer taps, less effort, less waiting, less thinking to reach their goal.
2. **Is it understandable?** — they always know what this is, what to do, and what will happen. No guessing.
3. **Is it taking them through hoops — or boring / annoying them?** — no detours, no tedium, no interruptions, no gimmicks that get in the way.
4. **Does the use itself delight?** — beyond not-annoying: responsive, satisfying, anticipating their needs.

If any answer is "no," it is not done — however beautiful it is.

---

## The two forces, and why neither alone is enough

Great design is two co-equal forces in tension:

- **The Concept (the look)** — astonishing to look at and feel. The trio's job.
- **The UX (the use)** — astonishing to use. This skill's job.

Beautiful-but-unusable fails (the gorgeous app no one can operate). Usable-but-generic fails too (the perfectly functional, utterly forgettable form). **You need both, and they are not naturally aligned** — the artful choice and the easy choice often pull apart (a stunning full-screen scroll-story vs. a user who just wants the price). The discipline is to make them serve each other rather than fight.

This is `architect-mentality` #7 — **refuse the false tradeoff.** "Beautiful or usable" is almost never a real either/or; it is a prompt to find the design that is *both*. The mechanism for finding it is the ping-pong.

---

## The ping-pong — the heart of this skill

**The use and the look refine each other in a loop, not a one-way check.** A concept is conceived, then pressured by UX (where does this make the user work, wait, guess, or detour?), then the concept is *re-conceived* to dissolve that friction *without* going generic, then pressured again — round after round until both are great. It is genuinely chicken-and-egg; what matters is that you *keep volleying*.

**The mission sets who leads — name it the *Lead Force*, decide it, record it.** Which force leads is not a vibe you carry in your head; it is a **decision** — *the Lead Force* — made from the mission and *written down* (in the brief, by `distill-the-brief`) so it is set once at the right altitude instead of re-litigated every round. Three settings:

- **Use-led (app-y)** (utility, productivity, transactional, safety-critical, accessibility-critical): the use leads, and **leads first** — before any world or hero is imagined. The opening volley is not a critique of an existing concept (there is none yet); it is where you **author the use** — the surface's primary verb, the regions and the job each must do, each act's outcome and far half, the real states (empty / first / in-progress / awaiting-the-other / error). That breakdown is a **contract the art must satisfy**: `envision` and `stage` give it a world-native form; they do not get to invent the surface's structure around a hero and leave the rest as filler. *Then* the concept makes it astonishing *without ever taxing the use*. `ux → concept → ux`.
- **Concept-led (dreamy)** (brand, marketing, art, immersive storytelling, a "moment"): start from the bold concept and feeling, then **pressure-test it hard against use** and re-conceive until it's also effortless. `concept → ux → reconcept → ux`.
- **Balanced**: a real back-and-forth, several rounds, neither permanently in the lead.

**Lead-first means binding, not merely earlier.** The reason a beautiful hero so often ends up ringed by an unpurposeful, un-appy screen is that the use force arrives *after* the art has already claimed the surface — so it can only critique the filler, never author a purposeful supporting cast. On a use-led node, flip that: give the use force a **generative seat at concept time.** It names what the *whole* screen must do — every region's job, every act's outcome, every state — and hands that down as a contract, so the art drapes over a complete, purposeful surface instead of backfilling the space around one staged moment. A check can reject filler; only a lead can prevent it. (This is the difference between owning the whole-screen *as a bar* and owning it *as a generator* — see the red flags.)

**Decide the Lead Force from the job, never the type.** What sets it is the **job in the moment** (accomplish a task under load vs. be moved/immersed), the **stakes** (money, safety, grief, conflict → clarity; brand, discovery, celebration → immersion), the **frequency** (daily tool → restraint; once-seen → spectrum allowed), and the **device & ergonomics** (thumb-bound, hurried, in glare). App *type* is only a loose prior, **never a lookup** — the same type spans the whole axis (Amazon vs. a perfume house; Wordle vs. *Journey*), so "it's a shop, so app-y" is the catalogue-by-reflex error the trio exists to refuse.

**The Lead Force composes — it is not one coarse dial.** It can differ surface to surface across a product, and even *within* one surface: a **dreamy heart can wear an app-y shell** (an immersive world whose *wayfinding* is a calm, findable app shell). So the question is never just "how dreamy vs app-y overall" but "which force leads *here*, in *this* part" — set per part, and let them compose.

**When to stop:** when both forces are great *and* neither can be improved without harming the other. If you hit a genuine wall where beauty and ease truly conflict, that is the signal to **invent** — a different concept or interaction that gives both (refuse the tradeoff) — not to quietly sacrifice the use. **You never settle below great UX to protect the art.** (If the mission is genuinely art-over-use — a pure art piece — say so explicitly; it is the rare exception, not the default.)

In practice: every time `envision` or `stage` produces a concept, run it through the four questions and the *reconciling art & use* cluster, feed what you find back into the concept, and repeat.

## Ask yourself

- Have I judged this by **how it's used**, not only how it looks — run the four questions (easy? understandable? hoops/boring/annoying? delightful) on the real person and device?
- Did I **ping-pong** the concept against UX — or just conceive something beautiful and hope it's usable? How many rounds, and did *both* end up great?
- Did I read the brief's **Lead Force** (use-led / art-led / balanced) and let it set the order — decided from the *job, stakes, frequency, device*, **not** the app *type* — rather than defaulting to art-first by reflex? And did I check whether the lead **differs within the surface** (a dreamy heart can wear an app-y shell)?
- On a **use-led** node, did the use force **lead first and bind** — authoring the surface's primary verb, every region's job, each act's outcome, and the real states *before* the art was conceived — or did it arrive late and merely critique a hero that had already claimed the screen?
- Where does the artful choice **tax the use** (extra taps, waits, hidden actions, scroll-fighting, gimmicks)? Have I dissolved that *without* going generic — or quietly sacrificed the ease?
- Is this designed for the **real moment and body** (thumb, one-handed, distracted, bad signal) — or an idealized focused user at a desk?
- Have I designed the **real use-situation, not the abstract feature** — can I name each act's *trigger* (why here, why now), its *parties* (who acts toward whom, controlled *by* them, not a mode-toggle), what the actor *knows*, what the recipient *feels*, and the natural *sequence* — so a real person would actually do this, here, now, this way? (See `reference/embodied-and-contextual-use.md` → "The use-situation".)
- Does it feel **instant and alive** — reacting the moment it's touched, dragged things following the finger, never hanging (heavy work runs async/background), loading instantly (no/short-live splash, sized placeholders)?
- Is every change **continuous** — smooth transitions everywhere (routes, states, menus, lists, list→detail), no abrupt layout shifts as content loads, the mobile keyboard handled — so nothing ever makes the user ask "what just happened?"
- Did I pick the **lowest-friction control** for each input (visible-and-immediate chips/toggles over click-to-reveal selects for short lists), and does the process not even *look* tiring?
- Does any **important feature depend on user-maintained state** — value gated behind the user telling the app something and keeping it current? Unless this is a tracking app, derive/detect/default it instead; reserve user-set state for bonus, presentational extras.
- For each data type, did I **interrogate the friction of entering it** and design the right control — even **inventing a novel one** when no standard control is good enough (then validating it's truly usable and accessible)?
- When something goes wrong (validation, a failure), is the user told **what's wrong, why, and how to fix it** — and **carried to the problem** (scrolled into view / "take me there", the control focused) — never left guessing or hunting?
- Am I holding the **hard bar** — astonishing to use — or settling for "usable enough" because the design is pretty?

## Red flags

- **Concept never pressured by use** — a beautiful design shipped with no ping-pong; usability assumed.
- **One-way check** — "design it, then we'll UX-test it" — instead of a loop that re-conceives.
- **The use force arrives too late to lead** — on a use-led surface, the art conceived the structure first and the UX pass only got to critique the leftovers, so the supporting cast around the hero is filler the check can flag but never authored. The use-led opening volley must **bind before** `stage` invents art — naming the verb, the regions-and-jobs, the act-outcomes, and the states — not arrive after to tidy them.
- **Art taxing the use** — scroll-jacking, transitions that delay every action, an artful nav nobody finds, a hero that buries the CTA, motion between the user and their goal.
- **A beautiful mood that doesn't say what to do** — a screen (a *home/landing* most of all) that's evocative and lovely but leaves the person asking "what is this? what's the main thing? what do I do? is this interactive, or that?" No clear **primary action**, ambiguous **hierarchy**, unclear what's tappable. Beauty without this clarity is half a design; for a home or utility surface the *use* must lead.
- **Cluttered — the screen has no triage** — every element at the same visual weight, so the user must read *each* to learn which matter, sparking a stream of "should I read this? is that button important? **what does that circle do?** am I missing something if I don't read that?" Even with honest labels and a real focal point *per element*, too much asking to be read *at once* exhausts. Calm is the default; density is earned. The fix is a **screen-level read-hierarchy** (one thing to attend to first, everything else visibly secondary or ignorable), **no ambiguous-purpose tokens** (every eye-catching shape is self-evidently a control, content, or plain decoration — never an undecodable dot), and **progressive disclosure of whatever this moment isn't about**. (See `reference/clarity-and-cognitive-load.md` → "Calm by default".)
- **Disclosure that dims instead of removes** — "progressive" disclosure that keeps every label present-but-faint so it *all* still reads at once and the eye must survey to triage, instead of *thinning* the resting frame (one thing leads, the rest collapsed/quieted, detail revealed on approach/dwell/tap). Faint is not absent. A tell: legibility bands of always-on text **stacked** round after round into competing tiers, rather than one staged header/eyebrow voice. (See `reference/clarity-and-cognitive-load.md` → "Calm by default".)
- **Placement for balance, not purpose** — a focal element set off-centre "to balance the layout," so it reads as *arbitrary* ("why is it there — is it even the focus?"). Placement must express hierarchy and function, never just compose the picture.
- **Internal jargon in the UI** — a label that's the *team's* behind-the-scenes concept, not the *user's* word ("rooms", "modules", "entities", "records", "items"). Labels are written in the user's language, for what the thing means *to them*; the internal metaphor stays in the design docs.
- **Mysterious affordances** — a vague control ("more →", an unlabelled icon, "all X") that hides unnamed functionality, so the user can't tell what they'll get. An affordance must be clear about what it does / where it leads; don't make them tap to find out.
- **Unsignalled interaction type** — an affordance that doesn't tell the user *what kind of interaction it is or what will happen*: will it **act in place** (do something now), **navigate** (go to another screen), **open a sheet/overlay** (stay here, reveal more), or **replace** content? An *action* ("Reach for them") and a *navigation* ("Play together" → that experience) styled identically leave the user unable to predict the outcome. Distinguish them — actions look/behave like actions (buttons that do), navigation looks like navigation (a destination you go to, with a go-affordance) — so the result is predictable before the tap.
- **Impress-once, annoy-forever** — a gimmick that delights the first time and obstructs every time after.
- **Hoops & tedium** — unnecessary steps, screens, modals, fields; making the user work or wait without reason.
- **Important feature gated on user-maintained state** — a core capability that only works once the user has fed the app some state and keeps it current (outside a tracking app, where logging *is* the concept); user-set state belongs only on low-value, presentational, or bonus features.
- **Designed for a desk** — ignoring thumbs, one-handedness, distraction, interruptions, and bad connections.
- **The abstract feature instead of the real act** — a capability modelled as a free-floating control rather than the lived situation it mirrors. Tells: a feature offered *out of the blue* with no real trigger (nothing happened that brings someone here to do this, now); a *directed* act between two parties wearing an **abstract mode-toggle** (or segmented control) to pick its direction, instead of being performed on the parties themselves (the relationship *is* the interface); every capability offered as an **always-available cold button**, ignoring the real sequence (one shares → the other responds). The fix is to design the *situation* — trigger, parties, what's known, what's felt, the order — not the pixels. (See `reference/embodied-and-contextual-use.md` → "The use-situation".)
- **"Usable enough"** — settling below great UX because the look is strong; or sacrificing the use to protect the art (outside a declared pure-art mission).
- **Lead Force read off the app *type*** ("it's a shop, so app-y") instead of the job/stakes/frequency/device — or treated as one coarse overall dial when the lead should differ across and within surfaces (a dreamy heart wearing an app-y shell).
- **Opaque/poetic action labels** — a label that names a *mood or gesture* instead of the *outcome*, so the user can't tell what a tap does or where it lands. The **plain-label / clear-outcome rule binds regardless of Lead Force**: art-led changes the *atmosphere*, never an action's readability.
- **An embodied gesture that is the mechanism but is never *invited*** — drag/press/draw is a real finger act (good), but the only cue is a dim whisper of micro-copy over a near-invisible object. The invitation must be a **visible, above-the-floor cue on the object itself**, not poetry floating in the dark.
- **The embodied act bypassed or trapped** — the meaningful gesture sits beside a visually dominant plain button that reaches the same outcome (the easy path wins the eye, the charged beat collapses to a click), OR it is the *sole* route to a core function (trapping anyone who can't drag). The embodied act must **be how the moment opens**, with a genuinely-secondary fallback that is never the only way.
- **A gesture that lands nowhere** — an embodied/signature act (drag, press, draw, place) that fires a transient glow or toast and decays back to rest with *nothing* selected, sent, or persisted: decoration you perform. The act owes an **outcome** — it must open, send, select, settle, commit, or leave an honest (even unscored) trace the user can see still changed. (Keep the embodied gesture the default on functional/use-led surfaces too — just make it *land*. See `reference/friction-and-flow.md` → "A gesture owes an outcome".)
- **A state that strands the user** — banking to a hush, a "flooded"/low mode, or any state that *removes the only way out* (deletes the nav, hides the exit). A state may quiet the world but must never take away the path forward; under-load exits especially live **in the thumb zone**.
- **A use-led shell rendered dreamy** — a wayfinding shell (even inside an art-led world) left as anonymous, unlabeled, scattered dim targets with no clear primary. The shell follows the *use* lead: self-labeling at rest, deliberate order, an obvious primary — the same app-y treatment its rooms get.
- **Hanging the UX / non-instant** — a tap that does nothing for a beat, a blocked UI during heavy work (instead of async/background), a draggable that lags the finger, a static splash where the skeletal UI could show.
- **Abrupt layout shifts** — content popping in and shoving the layout (CLS), hard route swaps, lists blinking instead of animating in/out, the keyboard hiding the focused field; the "what just happened? / where am I?" interface.
- **High-friction input by reflex** — a select for a 3-item choice, a wall of fields that *looks* tiring, the wrong control for the data, or the default widget dropped in without feeling the pain of using it (when a tailored or invented control would dissolve it).
- **Uninformative or hidden errors** — "Invalid" with no explanation; a submit that silently does nothing because an off-screen field is invalid; the user left to hunt for what's wrong instead of being told and taken there.
- **Generic-but-usable** — the opposite failure: easy and forgettable, no concept (this skill's partner failure; the trio is the other half — you need both).

> **Related.** The co-equal partner to the experience-design trio: `envision-the-experience` and `stage-the-vision` make a design astonishing to *look at and feel*; **this skill** makes it astonishing to *use* — and the two **ping-pong** until both are great, the mission setting who leads. `realize-the-vision` builds the reconciled result (and carries the build-side of UX — responsiveness, performance, accessibility — which are use, not decoration). `bespunky-product-ux:keep-users-oriented` is one facet of this force (orientation during waits and steps). A direct expression of `bespunky-engineering:architect-mentality` — *refuse false tradeoffs* (beautiful **and** usable), *design for the consumer* (the real person on the real device), *build for the goal not the brief* (they came to *do* something, not to admire a screen) — and, when reconciling concept and use forces a structural change, an `bespunky-engineering:architecture-first` redesign rather than a patch.

> **Resumability is a use-quality — make it the default.** A large, often-missed part of "easy / understandable / no hoops" is that the user **never loses their place or their work**: a copied or bookmarked URL lands them exactly where they were (the same module, opened item, dialog, edit/create mode, tab, filters), the **back button** does the obvious thing, and a **refresh** never wipes a half-typed draft or a chosen image. When you design a flow here, treat "can they get back to this, and did their work survive?" as part of the four questions — and build it through **`bespunky-engineering:resumable-state`** (the URL as source of truth for navigational state; working state deliberately persisted to a chosen home), not as a later pass. Losing place/work is friction the four questions must catch; resumability is how you remove it.
